## ACWR Overview

ACWR (Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio) is the most validated injury-prediction metric in running. It compares your recent training load (acute, last 7 days) against your established training capacity (chronic, rolling 4-week average).

Formula: ACWR = Current week km ÷ Average weekly km over prior 4 weeks

Sweet spot: 0.8–1.3 (adequate stimulus without excessive spike)
Caution zone: 1.3–1.5 (elevated injury risk, especially for tendons and bone)
High risk zone: >1.5 (substantial injury risk — this is the "spike" that precedes most overuse injuries)
Undertraining: <0.8 (you are detrained relative to your recent baseline; fitness may decline)

## Why ACWR Matters

Most running injuries are not caused by total volume — they are caused by sudden increases in volume. A runner doing 80km/week with a stable ACWR of 0.9 is at lower injury risk than a runner doing 40km/week who suddenly spikes to 65km one week (ACWR ≈ 1.6).

The chronic load represents tissue adaptation: tendons, bones, and muscles have adapted to sustain that level of stress. The acute load represents what you just threw at them. When acute exceeds chronic by >50%, the gap between stress and capacity produces injury.

Tendons are particularly sensitive to ACWR spikes. Achilles and patellar tendinopathy commonly follow ACWR >1.5 events. Stress fractures have also been linked to high ACWR spikes combined with bone stress.

## Calculating ACWR from Weekly Mileage Data

Given weekly km data (most recent first):
- Current week (W0): this week's km so far, projected to full week if mid-week
- W1, W2, W3, W4: prior four complete weeks
- Chronic load = (W1 + W2 + W3 + W4) / 4
- ACWR = W0 / Chronic load

If fewer than 4 prior weeks are available, use available weeks. With only 1 prior week, ACWR is less reliable.

## ACWR Limitations and Context

ACWR is not the whole picture:
- Sleep deprivation and under-fueling lower injury threshold even within safe ACWR
- Surface changes (road to trail, treadmill to concrete) add load not captured by km
- Speed sessions add more stress per km than easy running — the formula doesn't distinguish
- Individual variation is large: experienced runners tolerate higher ACWR than beginners

Caveats from research (Gabbett 2016, Carey 2017):
- Originally validated in team sports, not purely running
- The 1.5 threshold is a population average, not a hard cutoff
- A runner returning from injury has lower tolerance even if ACWR looks fine

## How to Use ACWR in Coaching

If ACWR >1.5: recommend immediate load reduction and flag to athlete that they are in elevated injury risk. Do not run additional quality work this week. Replace one run with cross-training or complete rest.

If ACWR 1.3–1.5: caution — athlete should monitor for early injury signs (localized pain, unusual soreness). Reduce next week's planned load by 10–15%.

If ACWR <0.8 for 3+ consecutive weeks: detraining is occurring. Gradually increase load to rebuild chronic base.

Ideal progression: keep weekly volume increases below 10% per week (see training_load.md), which naturally keeps ACWR in the safe zone.

## ACWR and the Coach's Role

When the athlete's data shows a recent weekly spike, always check ACWR before recommending next week's plan. Even if REI is high and HR metrics look good, tissue adaptation lags fitness adaptation by weeks — a runner can feel great and still be at injury risk if ACWR spiked.

The "feeling good" trap: athletes often feel best right after a training spike because fitness is temporarily elevated and they don't feel fatigued. Injury risk is highest 1–3 weeks after the spike, not immediately.
