## Daily Carbohydrate Needs by Training Volume

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for running at any intensity above easy jogging. Daily carbohydrate needs scale directly with training load.

Recommendations (ACSM/ISSN position stands):
- Light training (<1 hour/day): 3-5 g/kg body weight
- Moderate training (1-2 hours/day): 5-7 g/kg body weight
- Heavy training (2-3 hours/day): 6-10 g/kg body weight
- Very heavy training (>4 hours/day): 8-12 g/kg body weight

Practical example for a 70 kg runner doing 60 km/week: this is roughly 1-1.5 hours of running daily, requiring 5-7 g/kg = 350-490 g carbohydrates per day. This is substantially more than most recreational runners eat.

Inadequate carbohydrate intake produces measurable performance degradation that looks identical to overtraining in Garmin metrics: elevated HR drift, declining aerobic efficiency, cadence drop, and REI decline. Before diagnosing overtraining, rule out under-fueling.

Carbohydrate timing: prioritize carbohydrates in the 2-4 hours before a hard session and within 30-45 minutes post-session for glycogen resynthesis. Distribution throughout the day matters less than meeting total daily intake.

## Fueling During Long Runs

Glycogen stores support approximately 90-120 minutes of moderate-to-hard running before depletion. For any run over 75 minutes, exogenous carbohydrate intake preserves performance and reduces post-run fatigue.

When to start fueling: begin consuming carbohydrates at 45-60 minutes into the run, not when you feel hungry (by then, glycogen depletion has already begun affecting pace and form).

How much: 30-60 g of carbohydrates per hour for runs under 2.5 hours. For runs over 2.5 hours (or marathon racing), 60-90 g/hour is optimal if using multiple carbohydrate sources (glucose + fructose — different transporters allow higher total absorption). Single-source (glucose only) is capped at ~60 g/hour absorption.

Formats:
- Gels: 20-25 g carbohydrate each, most convenient. Must be taken with water (not sports drink).
- Sports drink (6-8% concentration): fuels and hydrates simultaneously. 500 ml/hour at 6% = 30 g carb.
- Real food: banana (27 g), medjool date (18 g), rice cakes with honey — all effective and gentler on the gut for some runners.

GI training: the gut must be trained to absorb carbohydrates during running. Start with lower amounts and practice fueling on long training runs — do not try a new fueling strategy on race day.

## Protein Requirements for Runners

Runners need more protein than the general population to support muscle repair, adaptation, and immune function.

Daily needs: 1.4-1.7 g/kg body weight for recreational runners training 4-5 days/week. Up to 2.0 g/kg for runners doing heavy training or strength work simultaneously. These figures are well above the RDA of 0.8 g/kg, which is a minimum for sedentary adults.

Post-run timing: consuming 20-40 g of protein within 30-60 minutes of finishing a hard run maximizes muscle protein synthesis. This window matters most after hard intervals, long runs, and strength sessions — not necessarily after easy runs.

Leucine threshold: muscle protein synthesis requires approximately 2-3 g of leucine per dose. Most complete protein sources (meat, eggs, dairy, soy) meet this threshold at a 20-30 g serving. Plant proteins (except soy) have lower leucine density, requiring larger portions.

Distribution: spreading protein intake across 3-5 meals (20-40 g each) produces better total-day muscle protein synthesis than one or two large protein boluses.

## Pre-Race and Race-Day Nutrition

The night before: eat a high-carbohydrate, moderate-protein, low-fat, low-fiber dinner. Pasta, rice, or potatoes with lean protein. Avoid anything unfamiliar, cruciferous vegetables, high-fat foods, or excess fiber — GI distress is a top race-day performance killer.

Race morning meal (3-4 hours before):
- 1-4 g/kg carbohydrate depending on race distance and individual tolerance
- Low fat, low fiber, moderate protein
- Examples: oatmeal with banana, bagel with peanut butter and honey, rice with egg
- Start hydrating — urine should be pale yellow before the race

30-60 minutes before: some runners benefit from 0.5-1 g/kg simple carbohydrate (sports gel, banana). Others experience reactive hypoglycemia from pre-race carbs — practice in training to know your response.

What to avoid: new foods, excessive caffeine (if not habituated), high-fat meals, dairy if lactose-sensitive, alcohol the night before.

## RED-S: Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport

RED-S (formerly "female athlete triad" but affects all genders) occurs when energy intake is insufficient to cover both training demands and basic physiological functions. It is more common in runners than any other endurance sport.

RED-S develops when caloric intake falls short of energy availability (EA): EA = calorie intake − exercise energy expenditure. Below 30 kcal/kg lean mass/day, hormonal disruption begins. Below 25 kcal/kg, performance and health impacts become severe.

**Metric-based warning signs in Garmin data:**
- Declining aerobic efficiency over weeks despite consistent training (AE worsening)
- HR at easy paces increasing over time (cardiac output declining)
- REI dropping without injury or illness explanation
- HR drift increasing run-to-run (fatigue accumulating faster)
- Cadence dropping at same effort (neuromuscular fatigue)
- Worsening Body Battery recovery despite adequate sleep

Non-metric signs: stress fractures (especially in the foot or tibia), frequent illness, persistent fatigue, mood changes, loss of menstrual cycle in female athletes, poor sleep quality.

Coaching response: if RED-S is suspected, directly address nutrition — not training load. Reducing training load alone will not reverse RED-S if energy restriction continues. Recommend consulting a sports dietitian. Do not push harder training when these patterns appear.

## Hydration and Electrolytes

Sweat rate varies from 0.5-2.5 L/hour depending on intensity, temperature, and individual variation. The goal is to avoid >2% body weight loss from sweat (beyond this, performance decrements begin).

Pre-run hydration: drink 400-600 ml of water 2 hours before running. Urine color is the simplest hydration check — pale yellow = hydrated, dark yellow = dehydrated.

During running:
- Runs under 60 minutes in cool weather: water only is sufficient for most runners
- Runs 60-90 minutes: water + optional electrolytes if heavy sweater
- Runs over 90 minutes: electrolyte replacement becomes critical (sodium 500-1000 mg/hour for heavy sweaters)

Sodium: the primary electrolyte lost in sweat. Sodium drives thirst and fluid retention. Sports drinks providing 300-600 mg/L sodium are appropriate for most runners. High-sodium sweaters (white salt stains on skin/clothing) may need 1000+ mg/hour in hot conditions.

**Hyponatremia warning:** drinking excessive plain water during long runs dilutes blood sodium. Symptoms: nausea, bloating, confusion, seizure in severe cases. Most at risk: slow marathon runners who drink more than they sweat. Prevention: drink to thirst (not on a schedule), use electrolyte drinks for efforts over 3 hours.

## Caffeine for Performance

Caffeine is one of the most evidence-supported legal ergogenic aids in sports science. Effect size: 2-4% improvement in endurance performance across multiple meta-analyses.

Mechanism: adenosine receptor antagonism — reduces perceived effort, delays fatigue onset, increases fat oxidation (sparing glycogen).

Effective dosing: 3-6 mg/kg body weight taken 45-60 minutes before exercise. For a 70 kg runner: 210-420 mg (1-2 strong coffees or a 200 mg caffeine gel). Benefits plateau above 6 mg/kg; side effects (anxiety, GI distress, arrhythmia) increase.

Habitual use: daily caffeine users experience attenuated performance benefits. Caffeine naïve or reduced-intake states (abstaining 3-5 days before a race) can amplify the ergogenic effect. Not required — the evidence for cycling-off is mixed.

GI risk: caffeine stimulates gut motility. Runners prone to GI issues during races should practice caffeine use in training, not debut it on race day.

## Iron Deficiency in Runners

Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency in endurance runners, especially women and vegetarians/vegans. Running-specific foot strike hemolysis (red blood cell destruction from foot impact) increases iron turnover beyond what sedentary people experience.

Three stages:
1. Iron depletion (low ferritin, normal hemoglobin): performance begins declining
2. Iron-deficient erythropoiesis (low ferritin, falling hemoglobin): significant fatigue
3. Iron-deficiency anemia (low ferritin AND low hemoglobin): severe performance impact

**Garmin metric signatures:** unexplained HR increase at the same pace (cardiac output compensating for reduced oxygen-carrying capacity), faster HR drift (reduced cardiac reserve), lower REI, persistent fatigue despite adequate recovery. Looks identical to overtraining.

Prevalence: studies report iron depletion in 15-35% of female recreational runners, 3-11% of males. Vegetarian runners have substantially higher rates.

Coaching response: if progressive HR elevation at easy paces appears over 4-8 weeks without a training explanation, recommend blood test (ferritin + hemoglobin). Ferritin below 30 ng/mL in a runner is concerning even if not clinically anemic. Below 20 ng/mL warrants supplementation. This requires a physician, not just dietary changes.

Dietary sources: heme iron (red meat, poultry, fish) absorbs 15-35%; non-heme iron (legumes, dark leafy greens, fortified grains) absorbs 2-20%. Vitamin C consumed with non-heme iron dramatically improves absorption.
