## Overreaching vs Overtraining Syndrome

Functional overreaching (FOR): short-term performance decline from excessive load, reverses in days to 2 weeks with rest. Normal part of progressive training.

Non-functional overreaching (NFOR): more serious, takes 2-6 weeks to resolve. Requires significant training reduction.

Overtraining syndrome (OTS): rare, severe, takes months to years to fully resolve. Characterized by systemic hormonal and immune dysfunction.

Most athletes encounter FOR occasionally. The goal is to detect it early and prevent it progressing to NFOR or OTS.

## Metric-Based Early Warning Signs

The following metric patterns in the run data should trigger a recovery recommendation:

REI: A drop of 10+ points over 3-5 consecutive runs without an obvious external cause (heat, illness, new route with elevation) is a reliable overreaching signal. Single-run REI drops are noise; sustained drops are signal.

HR Drift (HRD): HRD above 8% on runs that previously showed under 4% indicates cardiovascular fatigue. The heart is working harder to maintain output as the run progresses — a classic sign of under-recovery.

Cadence CV: If cadence consistency (CadCV) climbs above 10%, neuromuscular fatigue is likely. A tired runner loses the ability to maintain consistent stride mechanics.

Aerobic Efficiency (AE): If AE worsens (value rises) over 4+ consecutive runs during a build phase despite consistent conditions, the athlete is not absorbing the training load. The body cannot adapt faster than it can recover.

Pace at same HR: If the athlete reports that easy runs feel harder than usual at the same HR, or pace drops significantly at MAF HR, this is a soft sign of overreaching before the metrics catch up.

## Non-Metric Warning Signs

Ask the athlete about: persistent muscle soreness lasting more than 3 days after a run, sleep disturbances (difficulty falling or staying asleep despite fatigue), mood changes (irritability, lack of motivation, apathy toward running), frequent illness (colds or infections), and unusual appetite changes.

## What to Recommend

When overreaching signals appear:
1. Do not catastrophize or alarm the athlete. Frame it as the body sending a signal that's worth listening to.
2. Recommend 1 full easy week: reduce volume by 30-40%, no intensity work, all runs at Zone 1-2.
3. Set a checkpoint: "Let's see how your REI and HRD look after 5-7 easy days."
4. Do not add any compensatory workouts — extra mileage to "make up" for easy days worsens the situation.
5. After the easy week, return gradually. First quality session should be at 80% of normal intensity.

## Distinguishing Overreaching from Illness

Illness typically shows as a sudden REI crash (not gradual decline) combined with elevated resting HR. Overreaching is more gradual. If the athlete has had recent illness, all metrics for 7-14 days post-illness should be treated as unreliable for baseline purposes.

## Staleness vs Overreaching

Staleness (boredom with training) can mimic overreaching in reported effort but metrics usually remain stable. If REI is holding steady but the athlete reports everything feeling hard, staleness is more likely than physiological overreaching. Recommendation: vary route, introduce a new workout type, short training break.
