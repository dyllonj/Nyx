## IT Band Syndrome (ITBS)

Location: lateral knee pain, sharp or burning, typically 1–2 km into a run and worsening. May also present at the lateral hip.

Root causes: hip abductor weakness (gluteus medius), excessive hip adduction during stance phase, rapid mileage increase, camber running (slanted roads).

Management:
- Acute phase (pain during runs): reduce run volume 50–70%, avoid hills and cambered surfaces. Maintain fitness with cycling (lower ITBS stress).
- Address root cause: hip abductor strengthening is the most evidence-based intervention. Key exercises: clamshells, lateral band walks, single-leg squats.
- Running modification: increase cadence by 5–8% (reduces hip adduction), shorten stride length.
- Return to running: pain-free walking → pain-free short runs → progressive volume increase. Full return often takes 4–8 weeks.
- Red flag: if pain is present walking, refer to physio — do not run.

Metric signatures: ITBS often shows as cadence drop (lower cadence = longer stride = more hip adduction), elevated CadCV (form breakdown as pain compensates). REI will drop when pain is affecting form.

## Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (PFPS)

Location: anterior knee pain, diffuse, worsening with downhill running, stairs, or prolonged sitting.

Root causes: quad weakness relative to hip extensors, excessive pronation, sudden volume increase, hard surfaces.

Management:
- Reduce downhill running first — downhill dramatically increases patellofemoral stress.
- Quad strengthening: terminal knee extensions, wall sits, step-downs (eccentric emphasis).
- Gait modification: slight forward lean reduces anterior knee stress, as does higher cadence.
- Surface: avoid concrete; treadmill or softer surfaces during flare-ups.
- Return protocol: pain-free on flat → add uphills → add downhills → full training.

## Achilles Tendinopathy

Location: posterior heel/lower calf. Pain worst in the morning (start-up stiffness), may ease mid-run then worsen post-run.

Two types:
- Insertional (at the heel bone): do NOT use heel drops in acute phase — they compress the tendon at the insertion. Avoid hills.
- Mid-portion (2–7 cm above the heel): responds well to Alfredson protocol (eccentric heel drops).

Alfredson Eccentric Protocol (mid-portion only):
- 3 sets × 15 reps, twice daily, 7 days/week, 12-week minimum
- Eccentric only: rise on both feet, lower on injured leg. Do this even if it causes mild pain (grade 2–3/10 acceptable).
- Load progression: start bodyweight, add weight in backpack as strength improves.
- Evidence: 70–90% success rate for mid-portion tendinopathy. Patience is critical — improvements begin around week 6.

Running during Achilles rehab:
- Running load should be reduced 30–50% during acute phase.
- Avoid speed work and hill running.
- Soft surfaces preferred.
- Maintain easy running if pain is ≤4/10 and does not worsen post-run.

Metric signatures: Achilles tendinopathy often shows increased GCT (the ankle avoids push-off), reduced cadence, and lower REI. If these appear without an obvious fitness explanation, ask about morning stiffness.

## Shin Splints / Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (MTSS)

Location: diffuse medial (inner) shin pain, along the lower two-thirds of the tibia. Pain at start of run, may ease mid-run, returns post-run.

Distinguished from stress fracture: MTSS pain is diffuse (>5 cm length); stress fracture pain is focal, point-tender at one spot, and does not ease mid-run.

Root causes: tibial bone strain exceeding remodeling rate — typically caused by rapid mileage increase, hard surfaces, worn shoes, excessive pronation.

Management:
- Reduce mileage by 50%; eliminate speed work.
- Cross-training (pool running, cycling) maintains fitness without tibial load.
- Return protocol: must be pain-free for 2 weeks at reduced load before increasing.
- Progressive return: add 10% volume per week maximum.

## Stress Fracture Warning Signs

Stress fractures are SERIOUS — running on a stress fracture can convert a partial to a complete fracture (tibial fracture requires surgery and ends a season).

Red flags requiring medical evaluation:
- Point-tender pain (one specific spot hurts acutely when pressed)
- Pain that does NOT ease after 5–10 minutes of running (unlike MTSS)
- Nighttime pain
- Swelling around the painful area
- History of rapid mileage increase + female athlete + low calorie intake (relative energy deficiency = elevated risk)

If stress fracture is suspected: STOP running immediately and recommend medical evaluation including imaging. Do not advise modified running — the athlete needs imaging.

High-risk locations: navicular (top of foot), femoral neck (hip), anterior tibial cortex. These require more aggressive management than typical fibular or other tibial stress fractures.

## General Injury Coaching Principles

The most common coaching error with injured runners: under-estimating return timelines and letting the athlete return to full training too fast because they feel fine.

Tissue healing rule of thumb: tendons and bone take 6–12 weeks to structurally adapt after load changes. Feeling better (pain reduction) happens faster than structural healing. Pain-free ≠ healed.

Cross-training during injury: pool running (deep water, no impact), cycling, and elliptical all maintain aerobic fitness effectively. A runner who cross-trains during injury loses far less fitness than one who rests completely. Prescribe specific cross-training volumes during injury modifications.

When to refer to a physio: any focal bony pain, any injury that does not improve within 2 weeks of modified load, any injury in which pain is worsening rather than stable, suspected stress fracture.
