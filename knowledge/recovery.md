## Recovery After a Marathon

The marathon is categorically different from shorter races in its recovery demands. The combination of glycogen depletion, muscle fiber damage, immune suppression, and hormonal disruption requires a structured recovery approach.

Days 1-3: Complete rest. No running. Walking is fine. Expect immune vulnerability — avoid large crowds and sick contacts. Post-marathon immune suppression is real and lasts 72-96 hours.

Week 1-2: No running. Light activity only — walking, swimming, cycling at very easy effort. Sleep is the primary recovery tool.

Week 3-4: Easy running only, no structure, 30-40% of pre-race peak weekly volume. Run by feel. If legs feel heavy, walk-run is appropriate. No HR or pace targets.

Week 5-6: Gradual return. Volume at 50-60% of peak. Still no quality work. Focus on consistency.

Week 7-8: Introduce one moderate effort run per week if feeling good. Volume back to 70-80% of peak.

Full return to quality training: typically 8-10 weeks post-marathon for most runners.

## Recovery After Half Marathon and Shorter Races

Half marathon: 1 week very easy running (no intensity). Full training return in week 2-3.
10K: 3-5 days easy. Resume normal training in the following week with slightly reduced volume.
5K: 2-3 days easy. Can resume normal training within a week.

## Post-Hard-Workout Recovery

After threshold (tempo) sessions: allow 48 hours before the next quality workout. Easy running in between is fine.

After interval sessions (VO2max work): allow 48-72 hours before the next quality session. The longer and more intense the interval session, the more recovery needed.

After long runs over 25km: treat the following 1-2 days as easy only, regardless of how fresh the legs feel. Long run fatigue is often delayed by 24-36 hours.

If REI on the run immediately following a hard session is 5+ points lower than baseline easy run REI, recovery from the hard session was incomplete. This is a sign to extend easy days.

## Sleep and Its Direct Impact on Running Metrics

Sleep is the most underrated training variable. Its impact on measurable metrics is significant and underappreciated.

Chronic sleep restriction (under 7 hours/night): raises resting HR 3-5 bpm, increases HR drift during runs (HRD rises), degrades cadence consistency (CadCV worsens), and reduces aerobic efficiency (AE worsens by 3-7%).

If an athlete's metrics are worsening without increased training load, ask about sleep before changing training. Sleep debt of even 1-2 hours per night accumulates over weeks and produces measurable performance degradation that looks identical to overtraining in the data.

Optimal sleep for runners: 7-9 hours. For athletes training more than 60km/week, 8-9 hours is often necessary for full adaptation.

## Active Recovery

Easy running (Zone 1, under MAF HR) on recovery days is superior to complete rest for most trained runners because it promotes blood flow and metabolic waste clearance without adding significant training stress.

The key is keeping it genuinely easy. If HR climbs above Zone 2 on what is meant to be a recovery run, the pace is too fast. A slow jog that feels almost embarrassingly easy is the correct effort.

## Nutrition's Role in Recovery

Post-run nutrition window: consuming carbohydrate and protein within 30-45 minutes of finishing a hard run accelerates glycogen resynthesis and muscle repair. A 3:1 carbohydrate-to-protein ratio is well-supported. Real food (banana + milk, rice + chicken) works as well as commercial recovery drinks.

Chronic under-fueling is a hidden cause of poor recovery metrics. If an athlete is restricting calories while maintaining high training volume, expect AE degradation, elevated HR drift, and eventually REI decline. This requires a nutritional intervention, not a training adjustment.
