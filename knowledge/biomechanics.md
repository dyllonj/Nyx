## Ground Contact Time (GCT)

Ground contact time is the duration each foot spends on the ground per step, measured in milliseconds. Shorter GCT generally indicates better elastic energy return and running economy.

**Typical values by pace:**
- Recreational runners (5:00-7:00 min/km): GCT typically 250-300 ms
- Trained runners (3:30-5:00 min/km): GCT typically 210-260 ms
- Elite runners at race pace: 140-200 ms

GCT decreases as pace increases — this is normal and expected. Comparing GCT across different paces is misleading; compare same-pace GCT across time or sessions.

**What causes high GCT:**
- Low cadence (the two metrics are inverse: longer air time at slow cadence means more time on ground per cycle when landing is soft)
- Weak plantarflexors (calf/Achilles) — reduced elastic recoil forces longer ground contact to generate propulsion
- Overstriding — landing ahead of center of mass creates braking force that lengthens contact
- Fatigue — as muscles fatigue, elastic recoil decreases and GCT rises

**GCT as fatigue indicator:** GCT rising over the course of a run is a reliable signal of form breakdown from fatigue. A GCT increase of >10-15 ms compared to fresh running at the same pace indicates significant muscular fatigue. When Garmin data shows GCT creeping up in the final third of a long run, the athlete was working beyond their current aerobic capacity for that distance.

**GCT asymmetry:** Garmin reports left/right GCT balance. >2-3% asymmetry is worth investigating — it often indicates a unilateral muscle weakness, compensatory pattern, or early injury. Persistent asymmetry should be flagged.

## Vertical Oscillation: Energy Cost and Optimal Ranges

Vertical oscillation (V.Osc) measures the up-and-down movement of the torso per step. Every centimeter of unnecessary vertical movement wastes energy that could go into forward propulsion.

**Energy cost of V.Osc:** approximately 1 cm of excess vertical oscillation costs 1% additional energy expenditure at the same pace (Heise & Martin, 2001 data). A runner at 9 cm oscillation uses ~1% more energy than an identical runner at 8 cm. This compounds — the difference between 7 cm and 10 cm is roughly 3% energy cost, equivalent to a meaningful pace differential.

**Typical values by pace:**
- Recreational runners at easy pace: 8-12 cm (Garmin reports this range commonly)
- Trained runners at easy pace: 7-9 cm
- Elite runners at race pace: 5-7 cm
- Below 6 cm: elite territory; don't use this as a recreational target

**Target by fitness level:**
- Beginner: reducing from >11 cm to <10 cm is a meaningful improvement
- Intermediate: 8-9 cm is a realistic target
- Advanced: 7 cm or below indicates good economy

V.Osc and cadence are tightly coupled — increasing cadence almost always reduces V.Osc without directly cueing it. See form_cadence.md.

**V.Osc as run quality indicator:** comparing V.Osc at the same pace across runs controls for effort. If V.Osc is 0.5-1 cm higher on the same route at the same pace, fatigue, poor sleep, or dehydration is likely the cause.

## Foot Strike Patterns: What Research Actually Shows

This is an area where popular advice has diverged significantly from research.

**The research consensus:**
- There is NO foot strike pattern that is universally superior for recreational runners (Larson et al. 2011, Hasegawa et al. 2007)
- Elite marathon runners are predominantly heel strikers at submaximal paces (~75% in Hasegawa's analysis) — the forefoot-strike elite is a myth
- Forefoot striking at slow speeds increases Achilles and calf load, increasing injury risk in runners not adapted to it
- Midfoot striking reduces knee joint loading at the cost of increased ankle/Achilles stress — a trade-off, not an upgrade

**What is problematic:** heel striking far in front of the center of mass (overstriding) — this generates a braking impulse regardless of which part of the foot lands first. The problem is WHERE the foot lands relative to the body, not WHICH part of the foot contacts first.

**Coaching implication:** do not cue runners to change foot strike pattern unless they have a clear injury pattern linked to it. Cueing shorter stride length and higher cadence produces the most benefit with the least injury risk, regardless of what the foot strike becomes as a result.

The "barefoot/natural running movement" popularized by Born to Run has not been supported by injury rate data — transition injuries from forefoot adaptation are common and real.

## Forward Lean and Running Posture

Optimal running posture involves a slight forward lean of the entire body from the ankles — NOT a bend at the waist.

**Research on lean angle:** approximately 5-10° of forward lean from vertical is associated with optimal running economy. This keeps the center of mass ahead of the base of support, reducing braking forces.

**How to identify forward lean issues:**
- Excessive backward lean (running "sitting down"): creates braking impulse with every stride, increases quad load, often causes anterior knee pain
- Excessive forward lean at the WAIST (bending over): compresses the hip flexors, reduces stride length, often occurs in fatigued runners

**Coaching cues for correct lean:**
- "Fall forward from the ankles" — lean the whole body, not just the upper body
- "Tall with a slight tilt" — think tall first, then apply the lean
- "Nose over toes" — this overcue is better than cueing "lean from waist"

The lean naturally increases with speed — sprinters lean significantly more than distance runners. For recreational distance running, a natural lean that allows efficient forward momentum without obvious backward tilt is the target.

## Running Economy Factors Beyond Cadence

Running economy (RE) is determined by multiple factors. Cadence and V.Osc are the most directly measurable via Garmin, but others are significant:

**Stride length optimization:** optimal stride length is self-selected naturally at any given pace for most runners. Artificially shortening OR lengthening stride length degrades RE. The 10% cadence increase intervention works partly because it corrects overstriding, not because shorter strides are inherently better.

**Hip extension:** powerful, complete hip extension at push-off is the primary determinant of propulsion. Weak or limited hip extension forces the runner to compensate with excessive forward trunk lean or shorter, shuffling strides. Hip flexor tightness (from sitting) limits hip extension in many recreational runners. Hip flexor stretching + glute strengthening addresses this.

**Arm swing:** arms are not passive — they counterbalance leg rotation and contribute to rhythm. Optimal arm swing: 90° at elbow, hands relaxed (not fists), elbows driving backward (not arms crossing the midline). Arms crossing midline increases rotational energy waste. Letting arms hang down reduces cadence naturally.

**Trunk stability:** excessive lateral trunk sway indicates core weakness. Each sway episode shifts the center of mass outside the base of support, requiring corrective force. Lateral core work (single-leg exercises, lateral plank) addresses this.

## Form Breakdown Patterns During a Run

How Garmin metrics change as fatigue accumulates is one of the most useful coaching signals — it shows where the athlete's current fitness limits are.

**Typical fatigue cascade (order varies by individual):**
1. V.Osc begins rising (elastic recoil from Achilles and calves declining)
2. Cadence drops slightly (stride rate declining as neuromuscular fatigue sets in)
3. GCT increases (less explosive push-off)
4. HR drift accelerates (HR begins climbing to maintain pace)
5. Pace begins dropping (athlete can no longer maintain form + pace)

**What the pattern tells you:**
- If V.Osc spikes early in the run (first 20-30%), the athlete is already fatigued going into the run (poor Body Battery, insufficient recovery)
- If GCT rises significantly in the last quarter of a long run, the distance exceeded the athlete's current muscular endurance capacity
- If cadence drops but HR stays controlled, neuromuscular fatigue is occurring before cardiovascular fatigue — strength training deficit
- If HR spikes but form metrics hold, cardiovascular limit was reached before muscular limit — aerobic base needs development

**REI in this context:** REI is a composite metric. A run where REI is 5+ points below baseline easy-run REI indicates the run was more stressful than intended or recovery was insufficient. Track REI as a trend, not single-run noise.
