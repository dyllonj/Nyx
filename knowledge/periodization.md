## Polarized vs Pyramidal vs Threshold Training Models

Three dominant intensity distribution models exist for endurance runners. The evidence favors polarized for most trained runners.

**Polarized training (Stephen Seiler, 2010, 2013):** ~80% of sessions in Zone 1 (low intensity, below LT1), ~20% in Zone 3 (high intensity, above LT2). Almost no Zone 2 (moderate intensity). Elite endurance athletes across sports spontaneously converge on this distribution when volume is high. Seiler's studies of Norwegian cross-country skiers and rowers showed that athletes who drifted toward Zone 2 (the "moderate intensity trap") had worse long-term adaptations than those maintaining the polarized split. For runners, this means easy days are genuinely easy — not "moderate" — and hard days are genuinely hard.

**Pyramidal training:** High volume at low intensity, moderate volume at medium intensity, small volume at high intensity — a true pyramid. Common in high-volume programs. The difference from polarized is the intentional Zone 2 component. Appropriate for very high-volume runners (>100 km/week) where Zone 2 naturally accumulates during long runs and doubles.

**Threshold training (Veronique Billat):** Higher proportion of time at or near LT2 — the "Kenyan model." Works well for elite East African runners who have extraordinary lactate clearance capacity developed over years. For recreational runners with limited training history, this approach often leads to chronic fatigue without commensurate adaptation.

**Practical recommendation for recreational runners:** Polarized is the safest and most evidence-backed approach. Most recreational runners instinctively run their easy days too fast (Zone 2 / "gray zone"), accumulating fatigue without sufficient aerobic stimulus. If your Garmin data shows easy runs with HR consistently above 75% max HR, you are in the gray zone. Fix: slow down dramatically on easy days until HR is under 75% (Garmin Zone 1-2 boundary).

## Block Periodization vs Linear Periodization

**Linear periodization:** Gradual, simultaneous development of all qualities (base, threshold, speed) over a long training cycle. Progression is slow and steady. Traditional marathon programs (20-week buildup) are typically linear.

**Block periodization (Vladimir Issurin):** Concentrate one or two training objectives per 3-6 week block, cycling through emphasis phases. Example 3-block structure:
- Block 1 (Accumulation, 4-6 weeks): high volume, low intensity, aerobic base and general strength
- Block 2 (Transmutation, 3-4 weeks): reduce volume 20%, introduce threshold and tempo work, convert aerobic base to race-specific fitness
- Block 3 (Realization, 2-3 weeks): low volume, high intensity, sharpen speed, taper into race

**For recreational runners:** Linear periodization with a defined base phase, build phase, and taper is more practical than formal block periodization. Block periodization's advantages (avoiding interference between qualities, maximizing supercompensation) are most pronounced for athletes training 10+ hours/week. At lower volumes, concurrent development of all qualities is feasible.

**Key insight:** Whatever model is used, the base phase (aerobic development) cannot be compressed. Attempting to introduce high-intensity work before adequate aerobic base produces injury, illness, and stagnant performance. Minimum 8-12 weeks of easy-only running before introducing threshold work for new runners.

## Base Building Phase

The base phase develops the aerobic engine — capillary density, mitochondrial density, fat oxidation efficiency, and connective tissue resilience. It cannot be shortcut.

Duration: 8-16 weeks depending on experience. New runners: 12-16 weeks easy only. Experienced runners returning from a break: 8-12 weeks. Experienced runners in continuous training: 6-8 weeks between cycles.

Volume progression: 10% increase per week maximum (see training_load.md), with a cutback week every 4th week (reduce volume 20-30%). Do not increase both volume AND intensity simultaneously.

Intensity: 80-90% of all running in Zone 1-2 (below LT1, conversational pace). Long runs should also be easy — the common mistake of running long runs "moderately hard" defeats the base-building purpose and adds excessive fatigue.

Strides: 4-8 × 20-second accelerations at the end of 2-3 easy runs per week are appropriate in the base phase. They maintain neuromuscular speed without adding meaningful aerobic load.

Indicators of a good base: pace at the same HR improves by 15-30 seconds/km over 12 weeks; easy runs feel genuinely easy at paces that used to feel moderate; long run recovery improves.

## Peaking for a Race

Build phase (8-12 weeks before race): introduce one quality session per week — threshold (T-pace) runs starting at 20 minutes, building to 40 minutes of continuous T-pace effort by week 8-10. Keep easy volume high.

Sharpening phase (4-6 weeks before race): add interval work (I-pace) once per week. Two quality sessions per week maximum (T + I). Reduce easy volume by 10-15% to accommodate intensity.

Taper phase: see race_taper.md for specific protocols. General principle: reduce volume 30-50% over 2-3 weeks while maintaining intensity. Don't eliminate hard sessions — just shorten them.

For marathon specifically: the long run is the primary quality session. Peak long run 28-32 km (not 35+ km) for most recreational runners. Run at easy pace, not marathon pace. The long run's purpose is aerobic stimulus and glycogen depletion adaptation, not race-pace practice.

## Masters Runners (40+) Periodization

Masters runners (40+) require specific periodization adjustments due to slower recovery, reduced hormonal recovery response, and increased injury susceptibility.

Recovery between hard sessions: extend from 48 hours to 72+ hours between quality sessions. Most masters runners perform best with one quality session per week rather than two.

Volume ceiling: masters runners often need to cap total weekly volume 10-20% below their younger peak. Attempting to maintain 40-year-old volume in a 55-year-old body typically leads to injury. Quality over quantity applies more here than at any other age.

Base phase extension: masters runners benefit from longer base phases (12-16 weeks) before introducing intensity. The connective tissue adaptation that younger runners achieve in 8 weeks may take 12-16 weeks at 50+.

Strength training becomes more important with age: age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia) begins in the 30s and accelerates post-50. Heavy strength training 2x/week is non-negotiable for masters runners to maintain running economy and injury resistance.

Hormonal context: declining testosterone and estrogen reduce recovery rate and connective tissue resilience. Sleep optimization (see hrv_body_battery.md) becomes even more critical. Masters runners who prioritize 8-9 hours of sleep show dramatically better training adaptation than those sleeping 6-7 hours.
