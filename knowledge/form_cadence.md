## Why Cadence Matters — The Biomechanics

Cadence (steps per minute) is the single most accessible form metric because it's directly measured by the watch. Higher cadence at the same pace means shorter strides, which means the foot lands closer to the body's center of mass. When the foot lands ahead of the center of mass (overstriding), it acts as a brake — decelerating the runner with every step and increasing impact force.

Research shows that increasing cadence by 5-10% reduces vertical oscillation, ground contact time, and knee joint loading simultaneously, without requiring the runner to consciously think about any of those metrics.

Cadence target: the research does NOT support a universal 180 spm target. The correct evidence-based approach is a relative increase of 5–10% from each individual's natural self-selected cadence (Heiderscheit et al. 2011). A runner at 160 spm benefits from targeting 168–176 spm. A runner already at 175 spm gains little from pushing to 180.

The "180 spm" figure originates from Jack Daniels' observations of elite runners, but elite cadence reflects adaptation to high volume over many years, not a trainable short-term target for recreational runners. What research does show: any cadence increase of 5–10% reduces knee loading, GCT, and vertical oscillation relative to each person's baseline.

Practical frame: if your cadence is below 165 spm, increasing it will likely improve efficiency. Above 170 spm, the marginal benefit decreases and the correct focus shifts to other form elements (forward lean, hip extension).

## Cadence and Vertical Oscillation Connection

High vertical oscillation (V.Osc) and low cadence almost always appear together. They are two symptoms of the same root cause: overstriding. When cadence increases, V.Osc typically drops 0.5-1.5cm within 2-4 weeks without any explicit coaching cue for oscillation. Fix cadence first — oscillation often corrects itself.

If V.Osc remains high after cadence improves, the issue is likely hip extension and forward lean rather than stride length.

## How to Improve Cadence Safely

Never increase cadence more than 5 spm at a time. A sudden cadence jump stresses different muscles (shorter, faster contractions use the calf and Achilles more than the quadriceps) and can cause Achilles soreness if increased too fast.

Safe protocol:
1. Establish current baseline: average cadence from last 5 runs
2. Set intermediate target: current average + 5 spm
3. Use a metronome app (free apps are available) for 1-2 runs per week at target cadence
4. Only apply cadence focus on easy runs — do not combine with intensity work
5. After 3-4 weeks at new target (when CadCV improves and it feels natural), reassess

Do not focus on pace during cadence training — hold the same effort, not the same pace. Pace may slow initially as mechanics change.

## Cadence Consistency (CadCV) as a Progress Marker

CadCV measures how variable cadence is throughout a run. A low CadCV (under 5%) means consistent, metronomic footfall. A high CadCV (above 10%) means the runner is varying stride rate significantly, which often indicates fatigue, terrain-induced disruption, or unresolved form issues.

As cadence improves with training, CadCV should also decrease. A run where cadence averages 175 spm with CadCV of 4% is better form than a run at 175 spm with CadCV of 12%.

## Signs of Successful Cadence Improvement

After 6-8 weeks of consistent cadence work:
- CadCV drops below 6%
- V.Osc decreases by 0.5-1.5cm
- HR at the same pace drops 2-4 bpm (less braking = less energy wasted)
- REI improvement of 5-10 points is typical
- Ground contact time (if measured) decreases

## Cues for Form Improvement

Mental cues that help increase cadence and reduce overstriding:
- "Quick feet" — focus on fast ground contact, not longer strides
- "Land quietly" — loud footfall = hard landing = overstriding
- "Thumbs past hips" — arm swing drives leg turnover; faster arms = faster legs
- "Tall posture" — slight forward lean from the ankles (not the waist), head up

What NOT to cue: "Take bigger steps," "Push off harder," "Lift your knees higher" — these cues tend to increase overstriding and vertical oscillation.
