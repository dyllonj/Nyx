## The 10% Rule — What It Is and Why It's Incomplete

The traditional "10% rule" says never increase weekly mileage by more than 10% week-over-week. It's a useful starting guardrail but research shows it's an oversimplification. The absolute change in volume matters more than the percentage: a 10% increase on a 30km base week adds 3km, while 10% on a 100km base week adds 10km — very different training stresses.

Better guideline: limit weekly increases to 10-15% AND cap single-week additions at 8-10km regardless of percentage. Use a 3-week build / 1-week recovery structure.

## Three-Week Build, One-Week Cutback Structure

The most evidence-backed periodization for injury-free mileage progression:
- Week 1: base volume
- Week 2: +10-15% volume
- Week 3: +10-15% from Week 2
- Week 4: cutback to 70-75% of Week 3 (recovery week)

The cutback week is not optional — it's when physiological adaptation occurs. Skipping recovery weeks is the most common cause of stress injuries in recreational runners.

Signs you need a cutback week sooner than scheduled: REI drops more than 8 points over 3 consecutive runs, resting HR elevated 5+ bpm for 3+ days, persistent leg heaviness that doesn't resolve overnight.

## Mileage Zones and Their Effects

Low volume (under 40km/week): Builds aerobic base, reduces injury risk, appropriate for beginners and return-to-running phases.

Moderate volume (40-70km/week): Sweet spot for most recreational runners targeting half marathon to marathon. Significant aerobic gains possible with appropriate intensity distribution.

High volume (70-100km/week): Requires at least 2-3 years of consistent running base. Benefits plateau unless intensity is carefully managed. More is not always better.

## Intensity Distribution and Volume

When adding volume, add easy miles only. Never increase both volume and intensity in the same week. The rule: quality workouts (tempo, intervals) should represent no more than 20% of weekly volume. As total volume increases, the ratio of easy miles should increase too.

## Returning After a Break

After 1-2 weeks off: return at 70-80% of previous volume, easy effort only for first week back.
After 3-4 weeks off: return at 50-60% of previous volume, 2 easy weeks before any quality work.
After illness: return only when fully symptom-free. Start at 40-50% volume, HR will run higher than normal for several days — this is normal.

## Long Run Guidelines

Long run should be 25-35% of total weekly volume, capped at 3 hours for most recreational runners. Beyond 3 hours, recovery cost exceeds training benefit for non-ultramarathon runners. Long runs should be run easy (Zone 1-2, MAF HR or below).
