## Body Battery Overview

Garmin's Body Battery is a proprietary readiness score (0–100) derived primarily from HRV (heart rate variability) data collected during sleep, along with stress and activity data throughout the day. On the Forerunner 245, it reflects overnight recovery quality.

Body Battery is based on rMSSD (root mean square of successive RR interval differences), the primary HRV metric for parasympathetic nervous system tone. Higher rMSSD = more parasympathetic activation = better recovery state.

## Body Battery Interpretation for Training

0–25 (Depleted): Do not run. Full rest or very light walking only. This indicates severe physiological stress — immune function, hormone regulation, and muscle repair are all compromised. Running will deepen fatigue, not build fitness.

26–50 (Low): Easy run only, 30–45 minutes maximum. Keep HR in Zone 1 (conversational pace). Do not attempt any quality work. This is the most common range after back-to-back hard training days.

51–75 (Moderate): Normal easy or moderate training. One quality session may be appropriate if metrics (REI, HR drift) have been stable. This is the typical rested range for runners training 5–6 days per week.

76–100 (High): Full training readiness. Appropriate for hard workouts, long runs, or races. A Body Battery above 90 on race morning is a positive sign.

## Body Battery vs Raw HRV

Garmin does not expose raw HRV data directly on the Forerunner 245. Body Battery is a processed score. Key differences:
- Body Battery includes stress and activity throughout the day, not just sleep HRV
- Body Battery is calibrated to the individual baseline over time — a "70" means different things in week 1 vs month 6
- Day-to-day fluctuation of ±10 points is normal; trends over 5–7 days matter more than individual readings
- Body Battery resets during sleep — a morning reading reflects the quality of the previous night's recovery

## HRV Context and Running Performance

HRV suppression (low Body Battery) predicts performance decrements of 2–8% in trained runners. More importantly, it predicts poor adaptation: training sessions done in suppressed HRV states produce less fitness gain per unit of stress.

Common causes of Body Battery suppression:
- Inadequate sleep (<7 hours, poor quality, fragmented)
- Alcohol (even 1–2 drinks suppresses HRV for 12–24 hours)
- Training monotony (too many consecutive hard days)
- Illness (HRV drops 24–48 hours before subjective symptoms appear)
- Travel across time zones (disrupts circadian rhythm and HRV)
- Life stress (work, emotional) — parasympathetic suppression is not sport-specific

HRV and REI (Run Efficiency Index): expect REI to be 3–8 points lower when Body Battery was <40 at the start of the run. If REI drops without an obvious training explanation, check preceding Body Battery data.

## Using Body Battery for Training Decisions

Morning-based decision framework:
- Check Body Battery before deciding on workout intensity for the day
- If Body Battery is trending down over 3–4 days even with normal training, reduce load by 20% and prioritize sleep
- If Body Battery stays below 50 for a full week despite rest days, investigate sleep quality, nutrition, or illness

Weekly pattern: Body Battery should oscillate — high after rest days, lower after hard days. A flat or steadily declining pattern indicates inadequate recovery between sessions. The goal is not maximum Body Battery every day but adequate oscillation showing that the body is recovering between stressors.

Body Battery and ACWR: these are complementary metrics. ACWR tells you about tissue loading over weeks; Body Battery tells you about nervous system and hormonal recovery in the last 24–48 hours. A runner can have a safe ACWR but low Body Battery (from poor sleep) and still have elevated injury risk and impaired adaptation.

## Caveats

Body Battery is not a perfect metric. It can be artificially high after light activity days even if underlying recovery is poor. It is less reliable when sleep is very fragmented (it may underestimate recovery) or when the athlete has a high resting HR from heat or illness.

Use it as one signal among several, alongside HR drift trends, REI trends, and subjective feel. Do not override good subjective form cues with a Body Battery number, and do not override clearly poor Body Battery readings based on subjective "feeling good."
